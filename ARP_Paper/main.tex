\section{INTRODUCTION}
Sound propagation in the ocean plays a central role in underwater positioning, navigation, timing and communication systems, which are applied in a wide range of fields from national security and exploration of marine resources to the study of the natural acoustic environment \cite{Huang}, \cite{Etter}. However, accurately predicting underwater sound propagation remains a complex task due to environmental variability, ray bending, boundary interactions and different attenuation mechanisms.

To solve this, different computational models have been developed. Ray-based methods, that model the sound waves as discrete geometric paths are widely used due to computational efficiency. One particularly successful model that uses a variant of ray-tracing called gaussian beam tracing is the \textit{Bellhop} model, which is a state-of-the-art model used in the field \cite{Porter}.

In this work, we created our own implementations of simple ray-based models and compared them to the advanced Bellhop model. In our implementations we tried two different approaches for both ray-tracing and spreading loss and evaluated their accuracy. In the comparison we focused on ray path accuracy, eigenray detection and impulse response at the receiver (i.e time and amplitude calculation of sound paths). The work was limited to a deep-water environment with long range (100km) between source and receiver.

\section{BACKGROUND}

\subsection{Sound propagation in the ocean}
In ocean acoustics, sound is propagated using the ocean as a waveguide, limited between the surface and the seafloor. Within this waveguide, speed of sound varies due to density, which in the ocean is related to static pressure, salinity and temperature. Many different empirical formulas for calculating the speed of sound have been proposed \cite{Huang} with varying complexities and application ranges, but for most cases the simplified Medwin empirical formula can be used as a an approximation \cite{Jensen}, \cite{Lurton}. In the Medwin formula, speed of sound ($c$, in $m/s$) is calculated as
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
c &= 1449.2 + 4.6T - 0.055T^2 + 0.00029T^3 \\
  &\quad + (1.34 - 0.01T)(S-35) + 0.016z,
\end{split}
\label{eq:speedofsound}
\end{equation}
where $T$ is the temperature, in °C; $S$ is the salinity and $z$ is the depth, in $m$.

Due to the variety of temperature, salinity and depth in the oceans around the world, underwater sound propagation is strictly dependent on the sound speed profile (SSP) that is used. A typical SSP in deep non-polar oceans has an unstable mixed layer near the surface and a stable, linearly increasing sound speed profile in the deep isothermal layer near the seafloor \cite{Jensen}. Between these regions exists the deep sound channel, where the speed of sound reaches a minimum. The \textit{Munk profile}, is an idealized SSP showcased in Fig. \ref{fig:munk}, which contains many features of deep-water propagation and is often used for demonstrations of deep-water propagation.

For determining the sound propagation paths, speed of sound is treated in the same way the index of refraction is treated in optics. The refraction of the propagation direction changes as the speed of sound changes in accordance with \textit{Snell's law},
\begin{equation}
\frac{\cos{\theta}}{c}= p,
\label{eq:snellslaw}
\end{equation}
where $p$ is a constant, $\theta$ is the ray angle and $c$ is the local speed of sound \cite{Lurton}. Sound rays will therefore bend towards regions with lower speed of sound. A demonstration of deep-water propagation paths using the Munk profile is shown in Fig \ref{fig:munk}.

\subsection{Reflections}
At the surface and at the seabed of the ocean, the waves will experience reflections. In general, when the medium is not perfectly lossless, the local sound speed $c$ becomes complex, and the reflection coefficient is therefore also complex. Each reflection introduces both an amplitude loss and a phase shift. The reflection coefficient $\pazocal{R}$ and transmission coefficient $\pazocal{T}$ at an interface between two fluid media can be expressed in terms of their specific acoustic impedances $\pazocal{Z}_i = \rho_i c_i / \sin\theta_i$ as
\begin{equation}
\pazocal{R} = \frac{\pazocal{Z}_2 - \pazocal{Z}_1}{\pazocal{Z}_2 + \pazocal{Z}_1},
\quad\quad
\pazocal{T} = \frac{2\pazocal{Z}_2}{\pazocal{Z}_2 + \pazocal{Z}_1}.
\end{equation}

When the two media have similar acoustic impedance ($\pazocal{Z}_1 \approx \pazocal{Z}_2$), the reflection coefficient is almost zero. Conversely, when medium 2 has a much larger impedance than medium 1, the reflection coefficient is close to 1, meaning that most of the energy is reflected.

\subsubsection{Surface reflections}
At the interface, the difference in acoustic impedance between air and water is so large that almost total reflection occurs ($\pazocal{R} \approx 1$). The surface can therefore be approximated as a perfectly reflecting boundary. However, this assumption breaks down in agitated conditions when wind and waves generate bubbles that scatter and absorb acoustic energy, leading to non negligible reflection losses \cite{Jensen}.

In deep water, this bending traps the acoustic energy, hence creating ray paths that travel long distances without ever touching the bottom and that only reflect from the surface. Such typical deep-water environments, where these effects are prominent, occur in all oceans at depths exceeding about 2000m \cite{Jensen}. In this case, sound propagation often occurs without significant bottom interaction, dominated instead by refraction and surface reflection.

\subsubsection{Bottom reflections}
Reflection from the ocean bottom is far more complex. Unlike the nearly perfect surface, the bottom is generally a lossy boundary composed of sediments and rock layers with variable density, sound speed, and attenuation. The impedance mismatch between water and seabed materials determines how much energy is reflected versus transmitted into the sediment.

Layered sediments further complicate this behavior, producing frequency- and angle-dependent reflection losses due to interference within the layers. Depending on the incident angle, thin or half-wavelength layers can be acoustically transparent, while quarter-wavelength layers can act as anti-reflecting coatings \cite{Jensen}.

\subsection{Transmission loss due to seawater}
\label{sub:transmissionloss}
Transmission loss describes the decrease in signal amplitude as it travels from a source to the receiver \cite{Jensen}. As sound waves travel in the ocean, their amplitude is progressively attenuated by various loss mechanisms contributing to the total transmission loss. While reflection loss and various scattering losses contribute to the total transmission loss, this section describes transmission loss due to propagation in the medium. The transmission loss due to the seawater can be divided into two parts: geometrical spreading loss and frequency-dependent absorption loss.

Geometrical spreading loss measures attenuation due to the signal spreading outwards from the source, assuming the medium to be lossless \cite{Jensen}. This spreading can in near field be assumed to be spherical, and is calculated as
\begin{equation}
    TL_{spherical}=20\log r,
\end{equation}
where $r$ is the horizontal range in meters. Due to the boundaries of the ocean surface and seafloor, the attenuation in far field can be expressed as  cylindrical spreading loss
\begin{equation}
    TL_{cylindrical}=10\log r.
\end{equation}
For a point source, we can make the near field assumption when the distance is less than the depth $(r<z_{max})$ followed by a transition region after which cylindrical spreading is applicable at longer ranges $(r\gg z_{max})$ \cite{Jensen}.

In contrast, absorption loss measures how much of the acoustic energy is absorbed by the medium and turned into heat. This is usually modeled by an exponential decay law with distance $x$:
\begin{equation}
    A = A_0 \exp (-\alpha x),
    \label{eq:attenuationdecay}
\end{equation}
where $A_0$ is the rms amplitude at $x=0$ and $\alpha$ is the attenuation factor. The coefficient $\alpha$ has some dependence on frequency, temperature, pressure, salinity, and acidity. However, a simplified expression depending only on frequency is considered sufficiently accurate for most problems in ocean acoustics \cite{Jensen, urick1979}:
\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        \alpha &= \frac{1}{8.69} \big[ 3.3 \cdot 10^{-3} + \frac{0.11f^2}
        {1+f^2} + \frac{44f^2}{4100+f^2} \\
        &+ \quad 3\cdot 10^{-4} f^2\big],
    \end{split}
    \label{eq:attenuationfrequency}
\end{equation}
where $f$ is expressed in kHz and $\alpha$ in dB/km. Equation \ref{eq:attenuationfrequency} shows attenuation increases with frequency, such that low-frequency waves are only mildly attenuated, making them highly effective for long-range underwater communication compared to electromagnetic waves. For example, a 100Hz acoustic signal would need to travel around 2200 km to be attenuated by -10dB.

\subsection{Volume scattering}
In addition to boundary interactions at the sea surface and seafloor, sound propagation in the ocean is affected by volume scattering from marine life within the water column. Volume scattering is characterized by a scattering cross section, defined as the ratio of scattered power to incident intensity on a unit volume. This scattering contributes to both attenuation of coherent acoustic signals and generation of reverberation that interferes with sonar detection.

At lower frequencies (below 10 kHz), fish with gas-filled swim bladders are the dominant volume scatterers \cite{Jensen}. The swim bladder presents a high acoustic impedance contrast with the surrounding tissue and seawater, causing strong backscattering. Above 20 kHz, smaller organisms such as zooplankton become the primary scatterers. The target strength of individual fish depends on fish size, swim bladder morphology, frequency, and orientation relative to the acoustic beam, with typical values ranging from $-$40 to $-$20 dB for individual fish at moderate frequencies \cite{Foote1980}. The swim bladder contribution accounts for 90-95\% of the backscattered energy.

Volume scattering strength generally decreases with depth, with a notable exception being the deep scattering layer (DSL). The DSL is a concentrated layer of marine organisms, primarily small fish and zooplankton, that migrate vertically in the water column following the diurnal cycle. This layer can produce scattering returns comparable in magnitude to those from physical boundaries \cite{Jensen}.

In acoustic propagation modeling, volume scattering introduces two main effects: attenuation of the coherent field through scattering loss, and generation of a diffuse reverberation field. The volume scattering strength $S_v$ is expressed in decibels and relates the scattered intensity to incident intensity per unit volume. For a layer of thickness $H$, the integrated column scattering strength $S_c$ can be computed by integrating the local volume scattering strength $s_v(z)$ over depth. These parameters are used in sonar performance prediction and acoustic propagation models for applications such as fisheries acoustics and marine mammal protection.

\subsection{Sound propagation models}
Underwater sound propagation can be described using several types of models, each based on different approximations of the acoustic wave equation. These models vary in their physical assumptions, numerical complexity, and suitability for particular frequency ranges or environmental conditions. This section briefly presents the most popular families of methods \cite{propagationmodels, Jensen}.

First, ray theory based models describe sound as energy traveling along discrete geometric paths, or rays. This approach is particularly effective when the acoustic wavelength is small compared to the scale of environmental variations, such as at high frequencies or over long distances. The use of ray-based models in the research community was motivated by their simplicity and computational efficiency. However, they neglect diffraction and interference effects, making them less commonly used when it comes to model low frequency phenomena \cite{hovem2019}.

Next, the wave equation can be solved using the separation of variables technique to isolate the vertical and horizontal directions. The vertical wave equation solutions are normal modes, or eigenvalues, that can be summed at the source and receiver to generate the full acoustic field, depending on the horizontal propagation.
Each mode behaves like a standing wave in depth and propagates horizontally with its own phase speed. This approach is particularly suited for low-frequency and long-range propagation, as it can accurately describe the interference patterns between modes. The main drawback of normal mode models is that they become too computationally expensive at high frequencies \cite{propagationmodels}.

Finally, as most of the problems solved in ocean acoustics involve a one-way propagation (i.e. from source to receiver) separating the wave equation into incoming and outgoing solutions leads to the
Parabolic Equation (PE) method. PE models can account for refraction, diffraction, and scattering, which explains why they are so widely used and popular in the research community \cite{Jensen}. The PE computational load increases with the square of the frequency, which generally limits its use to frequencies less than 1 kHz \cite{propagationmodels}.

In summary, each category of sound propagation model offers a different balance between accuracy, complexity and computational cost. The choice of the model is determined by the problem requirements.

\section{METHODS}
Three different underwater sound propagation models are compared in this work. The models \textit{Geometric ray tracing} and \textit{c-linear} are simple ray-based methods based on techniques represented in \cite{Jensen}. The \textit{Bellhop} model is a more advanced beam tracing program using open source code from \cite{Porter}. In the models geometric ray tracing and c-linear, different algorithms for calculating spreading loss were implemented. 

\subsection{Ray-tracing implementation}
For both the implementations of geometric ray tracing and c-linear, ray paths are computed by discretizing the arclength $s$ into small steps of size $\Delta s$ and updating the horizontal and vertical positions as
\begin{align}
    x_{n+1} &= x_n + \Delta s \cos\theta_n, \\
    z_{n+1} &= z_n + \Delta s \sin\theta_n,
\end{align}
where $\theta_n$ is evaluated at the current depth using Snell's law (Eq. \ref{eq:snellslaw}). The difference in the two implementations: geometric ray tracing and c-linear, is how the ray-tracing is calculated within a step. 

The environment is represented by a one-dimensional sound speed profile $c(z)$ that varies with depth $z$.
A simple Munk-like profile \cite{Porter} is employed to approximate realistic ocean conditions:
\begin{equation}
    c(z) = c_0 \left[ 1 + \epsilon \left( \bar{z} - 1 + e^{-\bar{z}} \right) \right],
    \quad 
    \bar{z} = \frac{2(z - z_0)}{z_0}.
\end{equation}
where $c_0 = 1500~\text{m/s}$ is the reference sound speed, $z_0 = 1300~\text{m}$ is the reference depth (the sound channel axis), and $\epsilon = 0.00737$ is a non-dimensional perturbation parameter controlling the strength of the sound speed variation.

After implementing a source and receiver configuration, calculation of eigenrays is done using a ray fan. Ray-tracing begins from the source, producing a certain amount of rays at even intervals between two angles, creating a fan shape. Each individual ray is traced until the $x$-coordinate is the same as the receivers. Eigenrays are detected if the terminal depth of the ray is close to the depth of the receiver, allowing a tolerance of 5 $m$.

\subsubsection{Geometric Ray Tracing}
The underwater acoustic propagation is first modeled using geometric ray tracing, governed by Snell's law. This simple method does not solve the full acoustic wave equation, but approximates the trajectories of sound waves as they travel through the horizontally stratified water environment. The trajectory of the ray is governed by the differential equations:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{dx}{ds} = \cos\theta \quad \quad
    \frac{dz}{ds} = \sin\theta,
    \label{eq: dsRelations}
\end{equation}
where $s$ is the arclength along the ray. The local angle $\theta$ can be recovered from Snell's law:
\begin{equation}
    \theta(z) = \arccos\!\left(p\,c(z)\right),
\label{eq:arccosSnell}
\end{equation}
provided that $|p\,c(z)| \leq 1$, where $p$ is the Snell's law constant.

At each step, reflections from the sea surface and bottom boundaries were handled by reversing the vertical direction of propagation:
\begin{equation}
    \sin\theta_{n+1} = -\,\sin\theta_n,
    \label{Perfectreflection}
\end{equation}
whenever the ray reached $z = 0$ (surface) or $z = z_{\text{max}}$ (bottom).

\subsubsection{Cell method: c-linear}
Cell methods refer to the practice of calculating rays by dividing the medium into smaller discretized elements with simple properties. This can be done for range-dependent mediums by dividing the medium into triangles \cite{Jensen}. A simple implementation of the cell method \textit{c}-linear in a range-independent medium is presented.

Assuming linear sound speed within a discretized step, the \textit{c}-linear method can be used for calculating ray tracing \cite{Jensen}. Compared to the method of geometric ray tracing, which interpolates the ray at a discretized step as a straight line from start to end, \textit{c}-linear calculates the step as a circular arc based on the linear variation in depth $g$ from the sound speed
\begin{equation}
c(z) = c_0+gz,
\label{eq:linearSpeed}
\end{equation}
where $c_0$ is the initial sound speed at the start of a step and $z$ is the depth.

From the local angle in Snell's law derived in Eq. \ref{eq:arccosSnell}, the change in angle over the ray path can further be derived as
\begin{equation}
    \frac{d\theta}{ds}= -\frac{p}{\sin\theta}\frac{dc}{ds} = -\frac{p}{\sin\theta}\frac{\delta c}{\delta z} \frac{dz}{ds}.
\end{equation}
Since it is assumed that the change of speed of sound over depth is constant, and using relations in Eq. \ref{eq: dsRelations}, the last two terms can be substituted as
\begin{equation}
    \frac{d\theta}{ds} = -\frac{p}{\sin\theta}g \sin\theta = -gp,
\end{equation}
which is a constant. A constant ray curvature $d\theta/ds$ signifies that the ray follows the path of a circular arc. The angle of the ray at the end of the step is
\begin{equation}
    \theta_{n+1}=\theta_n+\frac{d\theta}{ds}ds=\theta_n-gp \cdot ds,
\end{equation}
where $ds$ is the step length and $n$ is an arbitrary discretized step.

To calculate reflections, a boundary hit point within the step is calculated using linear interpolation if $z\geq z_{max}$ or $z\leq z_{min}$ and specular reflection is assumed as in Eq. \ref{Perfectreflection}.

\subsection{Transmission loss implementation}
In the implementation of transmission loss, three loss mechanisms where taken into account: geometrical spreading, absorption loss and reflection loss. Two different geometrical-spreading models were used in this work, and implemented into one of the ray-tracing models. The implementations are however not dependent on the ray-tracing algorithm and could be implemented into either one. Absorption loss and reflection loss was implemented identically for both models. 

For measuring the impulse response at the receiver, arrival time calculation is also needed. This is done within the ray-tracing algorithms for each step by multiplying the step length with the local speed of sound.
 
\subsubsection{Geometrical spreading}
\medskip

\subsubsection*{(1) Hybrid Spherical-Cylindrical Spreading (c-linear Model)}
In the c-linear ray tracer, geometrical spreading is modeled using
a hybrid approach: spherical spreading in the near field and cylindrical
spreading in the far field. The transition point is chosen as the depth of the ocean floor $R_{\text{tr}} = z_{max}$. The transmission loss due to purely geometrical divergence is expressed in decibels as
\[
\mathrm{TL}_{\text{geo}} =
\begin{cases}
20 \log_{10}(R), & R \le R_{\text{tr}}, \\[6pt]
20\log_{10}(R_{\text{tr}}) + 10\log_{10}(R - R_{\text{tr}}), & R > R_{\text{tr}},
\end{cases}
\]
where $R$ is the total acoustic path length. The corresponding amplitude factor is
\begin{equation}
    A_{\text{geo}} = 10^{-\mathrm{TL}_{\text{geo}}/20}.
\end{equation}

This model provides the $1/R$ decay in the near field and the
$1/\sqrt{R}$ decay associated with cylindrical spreading in deep-water
waveguides. However, although computationally efficient, this hybrid model assumes an idealized waveguide geometry and therefore cannot represent changes in ray density caused by refraction. For these reasons the method provides only an approximate estimate of geometrical spreading, especially in environments with strong vertical sound-speed gradients (e.g. in the case of a Munk SSP).

\subsubsection*{(2) Jacobian-Based Geometrical Spreading (Geometric Ray Tracing Model)}
In the geometric ray-tracing model, geometrical spreading is derived directly from ray theory using the Jacobian of the mapping between launch angle and spatial coordinates. Following \cite{Jensen}, the ray amplitude at the receiver is
\begin{equation}
    A_{\text{geo}} = \frac{1}{4\pi}\left|\frac{c(s)\,\cos\theta_0}{c(0)\,J(s)}\right|^{1/2},
\end{equation}
where $\theta_0$ is the launch angle, $c(0)$ and $c(s)$ are the sound speeds at the source and receiver, and $J(s)$ is the geometric spreading factor
\begin{equation}
    J(s) = \frac{r(s)}{\sin\theta(s)}\,\frac{\partial r(s)}{\partial\theta_0}.
\end{equation} 
Here, $r(s)$ is the horizontal range of the ray at arclength $s$, and $\partial r / \partial\theta_0$ is computed numerically by comparing the receiver crossing points of neighboring rays in the launch-angle fan. This formulation automatically captures shadow zones, focusing/defocusing regions, and the full structure of multipath propagation in a refracting ocean waveguide.

\subsubsection{Absorption loss}
Absorption loss was modeled as depending only on the frequency, using equation \ref{eq:attenuationfrequency} described in section \ref{sub:transmissionloss}. For each eigenray, the total geometric path length $L$ (in meters) is converted to kilometers then affected by the attenuation factor $\alpha$. Each ray was sent at a frequency $f=100$ Hz.

\subsubsection{Reflection loss}
Reflection of acoustic rays at the ocean boundaries is implemented explicitly during the ray-tracing integration. The sea surface is modeled as a perfect reflection boundary. Therefore the pressure reflection coefficient is
\begin{equation}
    R_{\text{surf}} = -1,
\end{equation}
which introduces a phase shift of $\pi$ (sign inversion).

The seafloor is treated as a fluid sediment layer characterized by density
$\rho_2 = 1900~\text{kg/m}^3$ and sound speed $c_2 = 1650~\text{m/s}$, while
the water column has $\rho_1 = 1000~\text{kg/m}^3$ and sound speed
$c_1 = c(z_{\max})$ \cite{Jensen}.  
The reflection coefficient for an incident angle $\theta_i$ is computed using
the standard plane-wave fluid-fluid boundary formula:
\begin{equation}
R_{\text{bot}}
 = \frac{Z_2\cos\theta_i - Z_1\cos\theta_t}
        {Z_2\cos\theta_i + Z_1\cos\theta_t},
\quad
Z_k = \rho_k c_k,
\end{equation}
where the transmitted angle $\theta_t$ follows Snell’s law.

\subsection{Bellhop}
Bellhop is a beam tracing program developed by Michael Porter as part of the Acoustics Toolbox \cite{Porter}. Unlike simple ray-based methods that treat acoustic paths as infinitesimally thin lines, Bellhop employs Gaussian beam tracing, where each ray has a finite width. This approach can reduce artifacts near caustics and boundaries that are problematic for pure ray methods.

The Gaussian beam formulation extends traditional ray theory by associating a complex beam width parameter with each ray. As the beam propagates through the medium, both its center trajectory and width evolve according to coupled differential equations. For a beam with width parameter $q(s)$, the evolution along arc length $s$ is governed by
\begin{equation}
\frac{dq}{ds} = \frac{c^2}{q} \nabla^2 c,
\end{equation}
where $c$ is the local speed of sound and $\nabla^2 c$ represents the second derivative of the sound speed profile. This formulation allows handling of regions where rays converge (caustics) without the singularities that occur in pure ray methods. At each receiver location, contributions from all beams are summed with appropriate amplitude and phase corrections based on beam geometry.

Bellhop's numerical integration uses an adaptive step size algorithm that automatically adjusts to ensure ray paths accurately land on discontinuities in the sound speed profile, such as at SSP points and boundary segments. This approach ensures proper handling of refraction at layer interfaces and specular reflections at boundaries. The integrator follows the standard ray equations derived from Snell's law in stratified media, with additional terms to track beam spreading and curvature.

\subsubsection{Configuration via .env Files}
Bellhop reads its environmental parameters from text files with the `.env` extension, following a standardized format used throughout the Acoustics Toolbox. These files specify the complete acoustic environment including the sound speed profile, boundary conditions, source and receiver positions, and computational parameters.

The boundary specification allows three main types: Vacuum boundaries (`V`) produce perfect reflection with no transmission; Rigid boundaries (`R`) enforce zero normal particle velocity; and Acoustic halfspace boundaries (`A`) model the seabed as a fluid halfspace with specified sound speed, density, and attenuation. For the acoustic halfspace option, parameters are given as depth, sound speed, shear speed (zero for fluid), density, compressional attenuation, and shear attenuation. These boundary models range from simple geometric reflection to frequency-dependent bottom interaction.

Bellhop supports multiple run types specified in the `.env` file: Ray tracing (`R`) outputs the full geometric paths of all launched rays; Eigenray (`E`) identifies only those rays connecting source to receiver; Coherent transmission loss (`C`) computes the complex pressure field by summing beam contributions with phase information; Incoherent transmission loss (`I`) sums intensities without phase; and Semicoherent (`S`) uses a hybrid approach. The choice of run type will determine both the computational cost and the physical interpretation of results.

\subsubsection{Coherent and Incoherent Summation}
Bellhop can perform both coherent and incoherent field summation. In coherent mode (`C`), the complex acoustic pressure at each receiver is computed by summing contributions from all beams with their relative phases preserved:
\begin{equation}
p(\mathbf{r}) = \sum_{j=1}^{N} A_j e^{i\phi_j},
\end{equation}
where $A_j$ and $\phi_j$ are the amplitude and phase of the $j$-th beam contribution, and $N$ is the total number of beams reaching the receiver. This will produce interference patterns including constructive and destructive interference, which are physically realistic for coherent sources and receivers at fixed positions.

In incoherent mode (`I`), only intensities are summed, discarding phase information:
\begin{equation}
I(\mathbf{r}) = \sum_{j=1}^{N} |A_j|^2.
\end{equation}
This approach is appropriate when modeling averaged fields over many source or receiver positions, or when the acoustic field has significant temporal or spatial incoherence. Incoherent summation produces smoother transmission loss patterns without the fine interference structure of coherent calculations. The simple ray-based methods presented in earlier subsections implicitly perform an operation similar to incoherent summation by counting ray arrivals without tracking phase.

\section{RESULTS}

All three models were tested using identical environmental parameters: a Munk sound speed profile with source at 1000m depth and receiver at 100km range and 1000m depth. A fan of 10,001 rays was launched between $-30^\circ$ and $30^\circ$, and the seabed was modeled as a sandy bottom with density $\rho = 1800$ kg/m$^3$ and sound speed $c = 1700$ m/s at 100 Hz.

\subsection{Eigenray Detection}

The three models found significantly different numbers of eigenrays connecting the source to the receiver. Both the c-linear curvature model and the ray parameter model detected 54 eigenrays, while Bellhop identified 24. This 2-2.3x difference reflects distinct detection approaches rather than modeling accuracy. The custom ray-based methods scan through a dense ray fan and flag any rays passing within a tolerance of the receiver depth. Bellhop uses a beam tracing approach with adaptive step sizing and beam convergence criteria, identifying only the energetically dominant paths.

Figure \ref{fig:clinear_rays} shows the eigenray paths for the c-linear curvature model, while Figure \ref{fig:rayparam_rays} shows the ray parameter model results. Both models capture the characteristic deep-water propagation pattern with rays refracting through the Munk channel. The non-bouncing eigenrays launched near horizontal angles ($\theta_0 \approx \pm 4-5^\circ$) remain trapped in the sound channel, while steeper launch angles produce rays that reflect from the surface and bottom.

\subsection{Ray Path Characteristics}

The non-bouncing eigenrays cluster into three groups based on launch angle. Rays launched near $\theta_0 \approx -4.7^\circ$ to $-4.7^\circ$ have path lengths around 100,215m and travel times of 66.66s. A second cluster near $\theta_0 \approx \pm 0.6-0.7^\circ$ shows slightly shorter paths (100,048m) with minimal additional travel time. Rays launched at steeper angles ($\theta_0 \approx \pm 9.8^\circ$) travel longer paths (100,737m) with travel times of 66.58s. The c-linear model also detected several bouncing rays with 2-3 bottom and surface reflections, reaching path lengths up to 111km and travel times exceeding 71s.

The ray parameter model found two additional eigenrays compared to c-linear, including rays with more complex bounce patterns. Both models identified an eigenray near $\theta_0 = 0^\circ$ corresponding to the direct horizontal path through the sound channel axis.

\subsection{Amplitude Predictions}

Amplitude calculations differ significantly between the two custom models due to their spreading loss approaches. The c-linear model uses simple geometric spreading, producing amplitudes between $-117$ dB and $-124$ dB for non-bouncing rays, with bouncing rays attenuated to $-150$ to $-191$ dB due to bottom losses.

The ray parameter model implements Jensen's Jacobian spreading formula, which tracks changes in ray tube cross-section along the propagation path. This produces more variable amplitude predictions: non-bouncing rays range from $-122$ dB to $-166$ dB, showing the effect of caustic formation and ray convergence. \section{DISCUSSION}

The comparison reveals that simple ray-based methods can capture the geometric structure of deep-water propagation but differ in how they handle amplitude predictions and eigenray selection. The c-linear method improves on basic geometric ray tracing by treating each integration step as a circular arc rather than a straight line. This accounts for the continuous curvature of rays in a stratified medium and produces smoother trajectories, particularly in regions with strong sound speed gradients.

The ray parameter method further refines amplitude calculation by tracking the Jacobian determinant along the ray path. This captures focusing and defocusing effects as ray tubes expand and contract. The large amplitude variance in the ray parameter model ($-122$ to $-166$ dB for non-bouncing rays) reflects realistic caustic behavior where rays converge and diverge. However, the method also shows some outliers with very high attenuation ($-166$ dB), which may indicate numerical issues near turning points where rays approach horizontal.

Bellhop identifies fewer eigenrays because it selects beams based on energy contribution rather than geometric proximity. The 24 eigenrays Bellhop found likely represent the dominant propagation paths that carry most of the acoustic energy. The custom models, by scanning through 10,001 rays with a fixed tolerance criterion, capture many weakly excited paths that contribute minimally to the total field. This distinction is important for applications: if only the arrival structure is needed, the custom models provide more detail. If accurate pressure field prediction is required, Bellhop's beam summation with proper phase handling would be more appropriate.

One limitation of the custom models is their treatment of boundary reflections. Both assume perfect specular reflection with phase reversal at the surface, which is realistic for the air-water interface. At the bottom, both models compute angle-dependent reflection coefficients using the fluid-fluid boundary formula with the incident angle from Snell's law. Real sediment layers introduce additional frequency-dependent losses and phase shifts from layered structure that are not captured by the simple halfspace model used here.

Both custom models include frequency-dependent absorption loss using Equation \ref{eq:attenuationfrequency}. At 100 Hz, seawater absorption contributes negligibly to transmission loss over 100km ($\sim$0.5 dB total), but at higher frequencies this would become significant. Both models neglect volume scattering from marine life and small-scale fluctuations in the sound speed profile, which would introduce additional spreading and loss mechanisms.

The computational performance difference between the methods is substantial. The geometric ray tracing method completes in under a second due to its simple integration steps. The c-linear method takes slightly longer because it computes circular arc parameters at each step. The ray parameter method, despite optimizations like pre-allocated arrays and receiver-only data tracking, runs slower due to the Jacobian calculation. Bellhop's adaptive step sizing and beam tracking make it more expensive per ray, but it launches fewer beams to achieve similar coverage.

\section{CONCLUSION}

We implemented two simple ray-based underwater sound propagation models and compared them to the established Bellhop beam tracer. The c-linear curvature method and ray parameter method both successfully reproduce the geometric structure of long-range deep-water propagation through a Munk sound speed profile. Both models identified 54 eigenrays connecting a source at 1000m depth to a receiver at 100km range, compared to 24 eigenrays found by Bellhop.

The difference in eigenray count reflects detection philosophy rather than accuracy. The custom models scan densely spaced rays and report all paths passing near the receiver, while Bellhop selects energetically dominant beams. For impulse response prediction, the custom methods provide detailed arrival structure but require careful interpretation of amplitude predictions. The ray parameter model's Jacobian spreading calculation captures realistic focusing effects but shows numerical sensitivity near caustics. The c-linear model's simpler geometric spreading produces stable results but misses fine-scale amplitude variations.

Both custom models include frequency-dependent absorption loss but neglect volume scattering. The boundary conditions use angle-dependent reflection coefficients but assume simplified halfspace sediment models. These approximations are acceptable for the 100 Hz, 100km scenario tested but would require refinement at higher frequencies or in shallow water environments. For accurate pressure field prediction including interference effects, methods like Bellhop that handle coherent beam summation remain necessary.

The work demonstrates that ray-based methods provide computational efficiency and physical insight for long-range propagation problems where geometric effects dominate over wave phenomena.

\section{Ray Parameter Model Implementation}

The ray parameter model uses geometric ray tracing with Jacobian-based geometrical spreading. The implementation computes ray paths, eigenray detection, and comprehensive amplitude calculations including absorption, reflection, and geometrical spreading losses.

\begin{verbatim}
function Full_model()
    close all; clear; clc;

    % FLAG TO CHOOSE GEOMETRICAL SPREADING MODEL
    % 0 = Jensen Jacobian model (complex spreading)
    % 1 = Simple spherical spreading A = 1/(4πR)
    % 2 = Hybrid: 1 km spherical, then cylindrical
    spreading_mode = 0;

    % Environment parameters
    env.max_depth = 8000;
    env.max_range = 100000;

    % Source parameters
    source.depth = 1000;
    source.launch_angles = deg2rad(linspace(-25, 25, 50000));

    % Receiver parameters
    receiver.depth = 1000;
    receiver.rng = 100000;
    receiver.tol = 5;

    % Acoustic parameters
    freq = 100;   % Hz

    % Storage
    eigenrays = {};
    eigenray_times = [];
    eigenray_absorption = [];
    eigenray_reflection = [];
    eigenray_arrival_angle = [];
    eigenray_geom_spreading = [];
    eigenray_indices = [];
    normal_rays = {};

    % Select exactly 100 normal rays for plotting
    num_normals = 100;
    normal_ray_indices = round(linspace(1, ...
        length(source.launch_angles), num_normals));

    % Store all rays to compute Jacobian-based spreading
    all_rays = cell(length(source.launch_angles), 1);

    % Main loop
    for i = 1:length(source.launch_angles)
        theta0 = source.launch_angles(i);
        [ray_path, segment_times, segment_lengths, ...
            bounce_types, bounce_angles] = ...
            trace_ray(env, source, theta0);

        % Store full ray path for later Jacobian computation
        all_rays{i} = ray_path;

        % Check receiver hit
        if abs(ray_path(end,2) - receiver.depth) ...
                <= receiver.tol
            eigenrays{end+1} = ray_path;
            eigenray_indices(end+1) = i;

            % Travel time
            total_time = sum(segment_times);
            eigenray_times(end+1) = total_time;

            % Absorption
            total_length_m = sum(segment_lengths);
            total_length_km = total_length_m / 1000;
            alpha_dB_per_km = thorp_absorption(freq/1000);
            TL_abs_dB = alpha_dB_per_km * total_length_km;
            A_abs = 10^(-TL_abs_dB / 20);
            eigenray_absorption(end+1) = A_abs;

            % Reflection losses
            A_ref = 1;
            for b = 1:length(bounce_types)
                if bounce_types{b} == "surface"
                    A_ref = A_ref * (-1);
                else
                    A_ref = A_ref * ...
                        bottom_reflection(bounce_angles(b), ...
                        env.max_depth);
                end
            end
            eigenray_reflection(end+1) = A_ref;

            % Arrival angle estimation
            dx = ray_path(end,1) - ray_path(end-1,1);
            dz = ray_path(end,2) - ray_path(end-1,2);
            theta_arrival = atan2(dz, dx);
            eigenray_arrival_angle(end+1) = ...
                rad2deg(theta_arrival);
        end

        % Store exactly 100 evenly-spaced normal rays
        if ismember(i, normal_ray_indices)
            normal_rays{end+1} = ray_path;
        end
    end

    % Geometrical spreading models
    dtheta0 = source.launch_angles(2) - ...
        source.launch_angles(1);
    c_src = sound_speed(source.depth);
    c_rec = sound_speed(receiver.depth);

    eigenray_geom_spreading = zeros(size(eigenrays));

    for k = 1:numel(eigenrays)
        idx = eigenray_indices(k);
        theta0 = source.launch_angles(idx);
        ray_main = all_rays{idx};

        % Intersection at receiver
        [r_main, theta_receiver, success_main] = ...
            range_at_depth(ray_main, receiver.depth);

        if ~success_main
            s_fallback = arc_length(ray_main);
            eigenray_geom_spreading(k) = ...
                1/(4*pi*s_fallback);
            continue;
        end

        % JENSEN JACOBIAN MODEL
        % Neighbor rays → dr/dtheta
        idx_neigh = [];
        if idx < length(source.launch_angles)
            idx_neigh(end+1) = idx+1;
        end
        if idx > 1
            idx_neigh(end+1) = idx-1;
        end

        dr_dtheta = NaN;
        for j = idx_neigh
            rayN = all_rays{j};
            [rN, ~, ok] = range_at_depth(rayN, ...
                receiver.depth);
            if ok
                dth = source.launch_angles(j) - theta0;
                dr_dtheta = (rN - r_main)/dth;
                break;
            end
        end

        if isnan(dr_dtheta) || ...
                abs(sin(theta_receiver)) < 1e-6
            s_fallback = arc_length(ray_main);
            A_geom = 1 / (4 * pi * s_fallback);
            eigenray_geom_spreading(k) = A_geom;
            continue;
        end

        J = abs((r_main / sin(theta_receiver)) * ...
            dr_dtheta);

        A_geom = (1/(4*pi)) * ...
            sqrt(abs(c_rec*cos(theta0)/(c_src*J)));
        eigenray_geom_spreading(k) = A_geom;
    end
end

% Supporting functions
function [ray_path, segment_times, segment_lengths, ...
        bounce_types, bounce_angles] = ...
        trace_ray(env, source, theta0)
    ds = 10.0;
    max_steps = 500000;
    x = 0.0;
    z = source.depth;
    c0 = sound_speed(z);
    p = cos(theta0) / c0;
    dz = sign(sin(theta0)) * 1e-3;

    path = zeros(max_steps, 2);
    time_segments = zeros(max_steps,1);
    len_segments  = zeros(max_steps,1);
    bounce_types = {};
    bounce_angles = [];
    path(1,:) = [x, z];
    n = 1;

    for step = 1:max_steps
        c = sound_speed(z);
        arg = p * c;

        if abs(arg) >= 1.0
            dz = -dz;
            arg = 2 - arg;
        end

        theta = sign(dz) * acos(arg);
        dx = ds * cos(theta);
        dz_step = ds * sin(theta);
        x_new = x + dx;
        z_new = z + dz_step;

        if z_new <= 0
            bounce_types{end+1} = "surface";
            bounce_angles(end+1) = abs(theta);
            z_new = -z_new;
            dz = -dz;
        elseif z_new >= env.max_depth
            bounce_types{end+1} = "bottom";
            bounce_angles(end+1) = abs(theta);
            z_new = 2*env.max_depth - z_new;
            dz = -dz;
        end

        c_local = sound_speed(0.5*(z + z_new));
        time_segments(n) = ds / c_local;
        len_segments(n)  = ds;
        x = x_new;
        z = z_new;
        n = n + 1;
        path(n,:) = [x, z];

        if x >= env.max_range
            break;
        end
    end

    ray_path = path(1:n,:);
    segment_times = time_segments(1:n);
    segment_lengths = len_segments(1:n);
end

function c = sound_speed(z)
    z0 = 1300.0;
    c0 = 1500.0;
    eps = 0.00737;
    zbar = 2 * (z - z0) / z0;
    c = c0 * (1 + eps * (zbar - 1 + exp(-zbar)));
end

function alpha = thorp_absorption(f_kHz)
    f2 = f_kHz.^2;
    alpha = 0.11 * f2 ./ (1 + f2) ...
          + 44 * f2 ./ (4100 + f2) ...
          + 2.75e-4 * f2 ...
          + 0.003;
end

function R = bottom_reflection(theta_i, depth)
    rho1 = 1000;
    c1 = sound_speed(depth);
    rho2 = 1800;
    c2 = 1700;
    Z1 = rho1 * c1;
    Z2 = rho2 * c2;
    sin_theta_t = (c1/c2) * sin(theta_i);
    if abs(sin_theta_t) > 1
        R = -1;
        return;
    end
    theta_t = asin(sin_theta_t);
    R = (Z2*cos(theta_i) - Z1*cos(theta_t)) / ...
        (Z2*cos(theta_i) + Z1*cos(theta_t));
end
\end{verbatim}

\section{C-Linear Curvature Model Implementation}

The c-linear curvature model uses discretized circular arcs based on local sound speed gradients. This implementation includes hybrid spherical-cylindrical spreading loss.

\begin{verbatim}
% Discretized c Linear Model
% Based on the c linear method described by
% Jensen on p. 209-211
% Uses local curvature R = c(z) / (g_local * cos(theta))

clear; close all; clc;

% Source/receiver
z_s = 1000;    % source depth (m)
r_s = 0;       % source range (m)
r_rec = 100000; % receiver range (m)
z_rec = 1000;  % receiver depth (m)

% Ray fan
angles_deg = linspace(-30,30,10001);
angles = deg2rad(angles_deg);
depth_tol = 5; % eigenray hit tolerance (m)

% Numerical stepping
ds = 30.0; % arc-length step (m)
max_steps = 5e7;
max_range = r_rec * 1.2;

% Boundaries
z_min = 0; % surface (m)
z_max = 8000; % bottom (m)

% Transfer loss
A0 = 1.0; % initial amplitude at source
f = 100; % frequency for absorption calculation
r_transition = z_max; % transition range

% Sound speed and derivative (Munk)
c0 = 1500;
z0_munk = 1300;
eps_munk = 0.00737;

c_of_z = @(z) c0 .* ( 1 + eps_munk .* ...
    ((2*(z - z0_munk)./z0_munk) - 1 + ...
    exp(-2*(z - z0_munk)./z0_munk)));

dc_dz = @(z) (2 * c0 * eps_munk ./ z0_munk) .* ...
    (1 - exp(-2*(z - z0_munk)./z0_munk));

% Ray tracing
eigenrays = {};

for ia = 1:length(angles)
    theta = angles(ia);
    x = r_s; z = z_s; t = 0;

    est_size = min(ceil(max_range/ds)*3, max_steps);
    rpath = zeros(1, est_size);
    zpath = zeros(1, est_size);
    tpath = zeros(1, est_size);
    rpath(1) = x; zpath(1) = z; tpath(1) = t;
    idx = 1;
    A_reflection = 1;
    step = 0;
    found = false;

    last_finite_idx = 1;
    second_last_finite_idx = 0;

    while x <= max_range && step < max_steps
        step = step + 1;

        % C LINEAR METHOD
        c_curr = c_of_z(z);
        g_local = dc_dz(z);
        kappa1 = -g_local.* cos(theta) ./ c_curr;

        % EULER STEP
        theta_new = theta + ds * kappa1;
        x_new = x + ds * cos(theta);
        z_new = z + ds * sin(theta);
        t_new = t + ds / c_curr;

        % REFLECTION HANDLING
        if (z_new < z_min) || (z_new > z_max)
            if z_new < z_min
                alpha = (z_min - z) / (z_new - z);
                z_hit = z_min;
            else
                alpha = (z_max - z) / (z_new - z);
                z_hit = z_max;
                c_at_hit = c_of_z(z_hit);
                A_reflection = A_reflection * ...
                    bottom_reflection(theta_new, c_at_hit);
            end
            alpha = max(0,min(1,alpha));
            x_hit = x + alpha * (x_new - x);
            t_hit = t + alpha * (t_new - t);

            idx = idx + 1;
            rpath(idx) = x_hit;
            zpath(idx) = z_hit;
            tpath(idx) = t_hit;
            second_last_finite_idx = last_finite_idx;
            last_finite_idx = idx;

            idx = idx + 1;
            rpath(idx) = NaN;
            zpath(idx) = NaN;
            tpath(idx) = NaN;

            theta = -theta;

            ds_rem = ds * (1 - alpha);
            if ds_rem > 0
                x = x_hit + ds_rem * cos(theta);
                z = z_hit + ds_rem * sin(theta);
                c_at_hit = c_of_z(z_hit);
                t = t_hit + ds_rem / c_at_hit;
                idx = idx + 1;
                rpath(idx) = x;
                zpath(idx) = z;
                tpath(idx) = t;
                second_last_finite_idx = last_finite_idx;
                last_finite_idx = idx;
            else
                x = x_hit; z = z_hit; t = t_hit;
            end
        else
            x = x_new;
            z = z_new;
            theta = theta_new;
            t = t_new;
            idx = idx + 1;
            rpath(idx) = x;
            zpath(idx) = z;
            tpath(idx) = t;
            second_last_finite_idx = last_finite_idx;
            last_finite_idx = idx;
        end

        % EIGENRAY CHECK
        if second_last_finite_idx > 0 && ~found
            i1 = second_last_finite_idx;
            i2 = last_finite_idx;
            r1 = rpath(i1); z1 = zpath(i1); t1 = tpath(i1);
            r2 = rpath(i2); z2 = zpath(i2); t2 = tpath(i2);

            if ( (r1 <= r_rec && r2 >= r_rec) || ...
                 (r1 >= r_rec && r2 <= r_rec) ) && ...
                 abs(r2-r1) > 1e-12
                alpha = (r_rec - r1) / (r2 - r1);
                z_at_r = z1 + alpha*(z2 - z1);
                t_at_r = t1 + alpha*(t2 - t1);

                if abs(z_at_r - z_rec) <= depth_tol
                    entry.theta0 = angles(ia);
                    entry.rpath = rpath(1:idx);
                    entry.zpath = zpath(1:idx);
                    entry.tt = tpath(1:idx);
                    entry.z_at_r = z_at_r;
                    entry.t_at_r = t_at_r;

                    % Calculate path length
                    finite_idx = find(~isnan(rpath(1:idx)));
                    pos_i1 = find(finite_idx == i1, 1);
                    path_len = 0;
                    if pos_i1 > 1
                        for kkf = 1:(pos_i1-1)
                            ind_a = finite_idx(kkf);
                            ind_b = finite_idx(kkf+1);
                            dr = rpath(ind_b) - rpath(ind_a);
                            dz = zpath(ind_b) - zpath(ind_a);
                            path_len = path_len + ...
                                sqrt(dr.^2 + dz.^2);
                        end
                    end
                    seg_dist = sqrt((r2-r1)^2 + (z2-z1)^2);
                    path_len = path_len + alpha * seg_dist;

                    % Absorption loss
                    path_len_km = path_len/1000;
                    alpha_dB_per_km = ...
                        thorp_absorption(f/1000);
                    ABSORPTION_abs_dB = ...
                        alpha_dB_per_km * path_len_km;
                    A_abs = 10^(-ABSORPTION_abs_dB / 20);

                    % Spreading loss
                    if path_len > r_transition
                        SLdB = 20*log10(r_transition) + ...
                            10*log10(path_len-r_transition);
                    else
                        SLdB = 20 * log10(path_len);
                    end
                    SL_abs = 10^(-SLdB / 20);
                    entry.path_len = path_len;
                    amplitudeLinear = A0 * A_abs * ...
                        SL_abs * A_reflection;
                    entry.A_at_r = 20*log10(amplitudeLinear);
                    eigenrays{end+1} = entry;
                    found = true;
                end
            end
        end

        if x > r_rec + 1000
            break;
        end
    end
end
